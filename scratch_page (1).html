A look at why gender diversity in corporate leadership isn't just a social issue — it's a business imperative, and why I'm building a career at its intersection.

My Why

Strategy Shapes the World. Women Should Help Shape It.

I'm Gabriela, a pre-business student at BYU pursuing a path in strategic management and corporate strategy. As a Brazilian woman navigating American business culture, I've seen firsthand how much perspective gets left out of the room when leadership lacks diversity.

This page is about the intersection of two things I care deeply about: the field of strategy — frameworks, competitive advantage, organizational design — and the underrepresentation of women within it. These aren't separate conversations. They're the same one.

My goal isn't just to enter corporate strategy. It's to be someone who changes what that room looks like.

"When women are in the room, the decisions are better. That's not opinion — it's data."
— McKinsey & Company, Women in the Workplace Report

The Case for Change

Why Women in Strategy Matters

These are the three reasons I keep coming back to when I think about why this issue deserves more urgency than it gets in business school curricula:

  1. The business case is overwhelming
    • Companies in the top quartile for gender diversity are 25% more likely to achieve above-average profitability (McKinsey, 2020)
    • Diverse leadership teams produce stronger innovation and broader market understanding
    • Boards with more women consistently outperform on long-term ESG metrics
  2. The pipeline problem is structural, not personal
    • Women represent over 50% of entry-level corporate roles but under 25% of C-suite positions
    • Strategy and finance functions have slower gender progress than HR or marketing
    • Sponsorship — not just mentorship — is what moves women into senior roles
  3. Students have more power than they think
    • Clubs like BYU's Women in Strategy Association exist to build the pipeline early
    • Choosing firms that publish transparency reports signals demand for accountability
    • The next generation of strategists sets the cultural norms of the next decade
