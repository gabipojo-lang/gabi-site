Women in Business & Strategy

A passion project exploring the power, challenges, and future of women in leadership — and what it means to be a woman building her career in strategy today.

Why This Topic?

Growing up in Brazil, I watched women around me work twice as hard for half the recognition. From my mother managing a household and a career simultaneously, to brilliant women I met at Fiocruz whose ideas often went unheard — I saw a pattern I could not ignore.

As I started building my own career — in sales, international services, and now pursuing Strategic Management at BYU — I became passionate about understanding the landscape for women in business. Not just to navigate it, but to help change it.

This page is my exploration of that passion: why women in leadership matters, what the data says, and what we can all do to move the needle.

The Case for Women in Leadership

Research consistently shows that companies with more women in leadership positions outperform their peers — in profitability, culture, and innovation. Yet, in 2024, women held fewer than 10% of Fortune 500 CEO roles.

The barriers are real: unconscious bias in hiring and promotion, wage gaps that compound over time, and societal expectations that still push women to choose between ambition and caregiving. But so is the momentum: more women are entering MBA programs, founding startups, and demanding seats at the table than ever before.

For me, studying Strategic Management is both personal and professional. Strategy is about making decisions under uncertainty — a skill women develop every single day navigating systems not built for them. That experience is a superpower. And I intend to use it.

3 Things I've Learned About Women in Business

Through reading, mentorship, coursework, and lived experience, here are the lessons that have shaped me most:

  1. Visibility is strategy — you must advocate for yourself
    • Speaking up in meetings is not arrogance; it's professional presence
    • Networking authentically opens doors no resume can
    • Asking for promotions and raises is expected of men — it should be for women too
  2. Mentorship changes trajectories
    • Finding a mentor who's been where you want to go is invaluable
    • Sponsorship (someone advocating for you) matters even more than mentorship
    • Pay it forward: once you rise, bring others with you
  3. Data matters — and it's on our side
    • Companies with gender-diverse boards have 36% higher financial returns (McKinsey)
    • Gender parity could add $12 trillion to global GDP (IMF)
    • Tracking diversity metrics forces accountability at every level

A Talk Worth Watching

Sheryl Sandberg's TED Talk, "Why We Have Too Few Women Leaders," sparked a global conversation about the internal barriers women face — not just the structural ones. This talk changed how I think about my own ambition and what it means to "lean in."
